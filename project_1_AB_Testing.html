Introduction¶

An e-commerce company is considering introducing a new version of a webpage.
The company is interested in running an A/B test to determine if the implementation of the webpage's new format will create more sales transactions than the old webpage.

After completing the A/B test, there is a dataset of 294,478 unique customers with information about:
the date/time of visiting the webpage , version of the page that was accessed (the control group received the old page; the treatment group received the new webpage), and whether a purchase was made.

Part II - A/B Test¶

In [29]:
df_clean = df_v1.copy()

$p_{old}$: The conversion rate (user accesses the old page).
$p_{new}$: The conversion rate (user accesses the new page).

The alternative hypothesis H1 is that the new page conversion rate is higher than the old page conversion rate.
The null hypothesis H0 is that the old page conversion rate is higher than or equal to the new page conversion rate .

Null Hypothesis $H_0$: $p_{new}$ - $p_{old} \leq$ 0¶

Alternative Hypothesis $H_1$: $p_{new}$ - $p_{old}$ > 0¶



Testing Null Hypothesis $H_0$¶

Under the null hypothesis $H_0$, assume that

  • $p_{new}$ and $p_{old}$ are equal, and
  • that $p_{new}$ and $p_{old}$ both are equal to the converted success rate in the df_v1 data regardless of the page.
    So, the assumption is:

    $p_{new}$ = $p_{old}$ = $p_{population}$

Next steps:

  • Simulate (bootstrap) sample data set for both groups, and compute the "converted" probability $p$ for those samples.
  • Use a sample size for each group equal to the ones in the df_v1 data.
  • Compute the difference in the "converted" probability for the two samples above.
  • Perform the sampling distribution for the "difference in the converted probability" between the two simulated-samples over 10,000 iterations; and calculate an estimate.

p_diffs = []
for s in range(10000):
    new_page_converted =  np.random.choice([0,1], n_new, p = [p_new, 1-p_new])
    old_page_converted =  np.random.choice([0,1], n_old, p = [p_old, 1-p_old])
    obs_diff_v3 = new_page_converted.mean() - old_page_converted.mean()
    p_diffs.append(obs_diff_v3)

The value calculated above is the p-value.
The p-value > 90.3% ( much greater than the 5% Type I error) is not statistically significant and thus we conclude that we shouldn't move away from the null hypothesis.
In other words, we fail to reject the null hypothesis that fewer people will complete a purchase after viewing the new page.



Another way for comparing the mean of two independent and normal distribution samples is . . .¶

... a two-sample z-test and calculation of the Z_score:

$$ Z_{score} = \frac{ (p{'}_{new}-p{'}_{old}) - (p_{new} - p_{old})}{ \sqrt{ \frac{\sigma^{2}_{new} }{n_{new}} + \frac{\sigma^{2}_{old} }{n_{old}} } } $$
  • $p{'}_{new}$ and $p{'}_{old}$ ares the "converted" success rates for the simulated sample.
  • $p_{new}$ and $p_{old}$ are the "converted" success rate for the two groups in the population.
  • $\sigma_{new}$ and $\sigma_{new}$ are the standard deviation for the two groups in the population.
  • $n_{new}$ and $n_{old}$ represent the size of the two samples (they are the same in this project).


Next step is to make a decision to reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis based on comparing these two values:

  • $Z_{score}$
  • $Z_{\alpha}$ or $Z_{0.05}$, also known as critical value at 95% confidence interval. $Z_{0.05}$ is 1.645 for one-tailed tests, and 1.960 for two-tailed test.

$Z_{score}$ is calculated at -1.31 and the $Z_{a}$=$Z_{0.05}$ is 1.645.
This is a right-tailed test, and because $Z_{score}$ < $Z_{a}$, we fail to reject the null hypothesis.
The p-value is similar to the one we calculated at κ. , which says that we have ~90% probability of observing the difference, given that the null is true.

Both the $Z_{score}$ and p-value confirm that we fail to reject the null hypothesis.
